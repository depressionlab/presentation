Obligatory Book Tie-In

Measurement and Profit.

James Vincent's Beyond Measure is about our obsession with quantifying the world. It traces how humans have used measurement to make sense of the universe, but warns of the danger of reducing complex human experiences into mere data points.

Measurement is never neutral. Whoever controls what gets measured, and how, holds power over everyone else.

An Inevitable Outcome

Surveillance captialism, a term coined by social psychologist and philosopher Shoshana Zuboff, is an inevitable outcome as we continue to prioritize metrics, measurement, and profit to its extremes. It is the process of taking a human being with complexities, flaws, and desires, and measuring them down to a spreadsheet of clickable behaviors to be categorized and sold to the highest bidder.

Section 01: The Philosophy

The "Free"
Web (Web 2.0)

Privacy is about what is taken from us silently.

When people talk about data privacy, it can seem pretty suspicious and obscure, but it's not actually about hiding any dirty secrets. Data privacy is better seen as resisting the capitalist commodification of our human experiences.

Data Minimization

The concept that a company should only collect the absolute minimum amount of data required to provide a service. If a flashlight app needs your GPS location, that should raise alarm bells.

Section 02: The Myth

"I have nothing
to hide."

There is a phrase that kills more privacy legislation than even the biggest tech lobby could dream of: "I have nothing to hide."

It's a comforting thought. It makes us feel safe. But what does it actually mean?

We all know what happens in bathrooms. It's not a secret. There are no classified documents protecting toilets and sinks. But you still close the door.

You don't close the door because you're doing something wrong; you close the door because existing in a space in which you’re constantly observed strips you of your dignity and humanity.

This is because you want privacy, not secrecy. Privacy is the boundary between being a human and being a specimen.

Section 03: Defining terms

Privacy is
about power.

You don't need to be a hacker in a dimly-lit room to care about privacy. Let's clarify some distinct concepts:

Privacy

Data is only seen by who you intend to see it. It is entirely about consent and control.

Security

You can trust the tools you're using. Your data is protected against unauthorized access or breaches.

Anonymity

Acting without being identified. Completely disconnecting your digital actions from your real-world identity.

You don't need all three all the time. Right now, on the modern web, you barely have one.

Section 04: Public Records

What do we
already know about you?

Before we get into complicated browser trackers, there is a category of data openly available to anyone with a browser and thirty seconds of free time—the exact data used in the phishing email we just saw.

What's available for free

  • Full name + exact age
  • Current & past home addresses
  • Phone numbers & emails
  • Family members' names (parents, siblings)
  • Marriage & divorce records

Where does it come from?

  • Voter registration records (public in most states)
  • Property/housing records (publicly accessible)
  • Court & DMV records
  • Old data breaches scraped and compiled
Section 05: Data Brokers

The People-Search
Industry.

People-search sites aggregate public record data into a single, highly readable profile. They require no login. They're designed for the average consumer, but they are routinely weaponized by stalkers, scammers, and abusers.

When we looked up a random adult, here is what came back instantly:

  • Full address history going back 15 years, including childhood homes.
  • Names of close relatives: parents, siblings, and adult children.
  • Associated phone numbers and email addresses.
  • Their employment history and current employer.
This provides enough detail to craft a convincing, highly personalized attack on anyone.
Section 06: Why is this legal?

A failure of
legislation.

In the US, there is no comprehensive federal data privacy law. Aggregating and reselling public records is entirely legal. The companies doing this have spent heavily to keep it that way.

In the EU, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) fundamentally changed this:

  • Companies must have a legal basis to collect your data.
  • You have the right to be forgotten.
  • People-search sites of this type cannot legally operate for EU residents.

The only reason you can download your Instagram, TikTok, or Google data today is because of global GDPR pressure.

Regulatory Landscape

EU
GDPR (2018)

Comprehensive federal-level data protection. Mandates data minimization, purpose limitation, right to erasure, and breach notification within 72 hours.

US
No Federal Equivalent

A patchwork of sector-specific laws (HIPAA for health, FERPA for education). No general right to erasure. People search sites are fully legal.

Tech lobbyists spent hundreds of millions to prevent a US equivalent to the GDPR.

Section 07: Real Consequences

This is not
an abstract problem.

Domestic Violence

Survivors attempting to rebuild their lives are findable in seconds by abusers using free tools. Confidential shelter locations are often compromised through family member lookups.

Stalking

Journalists, activists, and public figures can be physically located instantly. From popular creators to school teachers, no one is exempt from being mapped.

Targeted Scams

Armed with address history, relatives' names, and employers, scammers craft impersonations pretending to be a family member in trouble, a bank, or law enforcement.

Law Enforcement Bypass: Government agencies are explicitly purchasing data broker databases to bypass the need for warrants. If they can buy your location data from an app developer, they don't need a judge's permission.

Section 08: Browser Tracking

The invisible
follow.

People search is layer one. Layer two is the browser. Your activity across the entire web is tracked — not just on sites you use, but on sites you've never heard of.

How tracking works

  • Third-party cookies: Small files that follow you from site to site.
  • Tracking pixels: Invisible 1x1 images embedded in pages and emails that log when you open them.
  • Browser fingerprinting: Your screen size, installed fonts, and hardware config form a unique identifier.
  • Link decorators: The cluttered ?utm_source= strings at the end of URLs.

Who is doing it?

You are not just tracked while on Google or Meta. Their tracking scripts are invisibly embedded across news sites, health forums, and children's content.

When you read an article about a medical condition, search for a bankruptcy lawyer, or look up information about a protest, that information is logged and permanently added to your shadow profile.

Section 10: The Data Economy

Your data gets
sold.

What data brokers package

  • Browsing history and search queries.
  • Purchase history and estimated financial status.
  • Precise GPS location history (where you sleep, work, and visit).
  • Inferred health conditions and medical interests.

The data broker industry is worth over $300 billion annually. You are the product. You are not paid.

"But I like personalized ads!"

Advertisers do not have your interests in mind, they have conversion rates in mind. They use your behavioral profiles to fuel:

  • Planned obsolescence: selling products designed to break.
  • Impulse buying: triggered by emotional state profiling.

The "personalized ads" framing is a marketing spin designed to make surveillance feel like a helpful service. It is an invasion of privacy.

Section 11: Security Failures

And then
it gets stolen.

Even if you trusted every company handling your data — and you shouldn't — the massive hoards of data they collect are incredibly attractive targets. Breaches are a constant, ongoing feature of the system.

2024
National Public Data Breach

Nearly 3 billion records containing Social Security Numbers, addresses, and phone numbers were leaked. A stark reminder that data brokers fail to secure the data they scrape.

2023
Experian / 23andMe

Massive exposures of credit histories and highly sensitive genetic information due to credential stuffing and systemic failures.

2018
Cambridge Analytica / Facebook

87 million users' psychological data harvested without consent and utilized for highly targeted political manipulation.

Every company that collects more data than it needs creates a larger target. The only real solution is collecting less data in the first place.

Section 12: The Architecture

This is happening
by design.

The surveillance infrastructure was built intentionally to extract maximum profit from human attention and behavior. Surveillance capitalism refines your digital footprints into predictive products to sell.

Tech monopolies frame data collection as "improving your experience" while burying the truth in 10,000-word privacy policies designed specifically to be unreadable.

The lack of a US privacy law is the intentional outcome of an industry protecting its business model.

Who blocked privacy laws?

Google, Meta, Amazon, and massive advertising coalitions lobbied tirelessly against giving US citizens basic data rights.

Who gets hurt most?

People fleeing domestic abuse, undocumented immigrants, activists, and minors. Those with the least power to push back are the most exposed.

Section 13: Look For Yourself

What do they
actually have on you?

Because of the GDPR, major platforms are now legally required to allow users to download their personal data archives. Doing this is eye-opening.

How to request your data

  • Instagram: Settings → Your Activity → Download Your Information
  • Google: takeout.google.com
  • Apple: privacy.apple.com
  • TikTok: Settings → Privacy → Download Your Data

What you'll typically find

  • Every search query you've ever typed.
  • Precise GPS location history going back years.
  • A list of highly specific inferred interests, political leanings, and estimated income.

actually help.

Firefox / Brave

Proton Mail

Section 15: The Tool Belt

More tools
worth knowing.

Password Manager

Bitwarden

Free and open source. Generates and stores strong unique passwords to completely prevent credential stuffing after inevitable corporate breaches.

VPN

Mullvad / Proton VPN

Encrypts your internet traffic to hide it from your Internet Service Provider. Mullvad famously keeps zero logs and accepts anonymous cash payments.

Encrypted Messaging

Signal

End-to-End encrypted by default. Non-profit and open source. WhatsApp shares vast metadata with Meta. iMessage locks you into Apple's ecosystem.

Data Broker Opt-out

Remove Yourself

Services like DeleteMe or Kanary automate the grueling process of removing your profile from hundreds of data brokers for a fee.

Cookie Management

Reject Non-Essential

Always find the "manage preferences" option and disable analytics and advertising cookies. Never just click "Accept All" to clear the popup.

Secure DNS

Encrypted Lookups

Setting your device DNS to Quad9 or 1.1.1.1 encrypts your domain lookups, stopping your ISP from selling your browsing history.

Section 16: The Bigger Picture

Individual changes
are not enough.

Individual solutions to structural problems are inherently limited. Not everyone has the time, technical literacy, or resources to navigate this ecosystem. The people who can't remain hopelessly exposed.

The goal must be that surveillance capitalism is constrained by law, the exact same way other forms of human exploitation are constrained by law.

What political action looks like

  • Demand comprehensive federal privacy legislation (a US GDPR).
  • Oppose all legislation attempting to weaken End-to-End Encryption.
  • Push back on government agencies buying data broker access.

Closing

Measurement
as control.

Vincent's point in Beyond Measure applies perfectly to the modern data economy. You are being measured constantly, quantified flawlessly, and sold endlessly. Because the US has no meaningful privacy laws, you have almost no right to stop it.

Who should be in control of this measurement?

The massive corporations extracting infinite profit from it?
Or the human beings whose lives are being quantified?

Privacy isn't about having something to hide.
It's about who gets to hold the measuring stick.
