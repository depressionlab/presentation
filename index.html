Obligatory Book Tie-In

Measurement
is Power.

James Vincent's Beyond Measure is about our obsession with quantifying the world. It traces how humans have used measurement to make sense of the world, but warns of the danger of reducing complex human experiences and natural phenomena into metrics, algorithms, and data points.

Measurement is never neutral. Whoever controls what gets measured, and how, holds power over everyone else.

The Ultimate Act of Measurement

Surveillance is the ultimate act of measurement. It is the process of taking a human being with complexities, flaws, and desires, and measuring them down to a spreadsheet of clickable behaviors to be sold to the highest bidder.

Section 01: The Philosophy

The "Free"
Web.

Privacy isn't just about what we post willingly, but what is taken from us silently.

Data privacy is not about hiding secrets. Data privacy is about resisting the capitalist commodification of our human experiences.

When we are tracked, measured, and categorized, we are stripped of our autonomy and turned into products.

Every time we talk about a tracker or a data broker, we should frame it as an act of measurement imposed on humans for someone else’s profit.

Section 02: The Myth

"I have nothing
to hide."

There is a phrase that kills more privacy legislation than even the biggest tech lobby could dream of: “I have nothing to hide.”

It's a comforting thought. It makes us feel safe. But what does it actually mean?

We all know what happens in bathrooms. It's not a secret. There are no classified documents protecting toilets and sinks. But you still close the door.

You don't close the door because you're doing something wrong; you close the door because existing in a space in which you’re constantly observed strips you of your humanity.

This is because you want privacy, not secrecy. Privacy is the boundary between being a human and being a specimen.

There are always certain facts about us (personal health information, sexual behavior) that we wouldn’t want the whole world to know. The need for privacy is legitimate. Privacy is about empowering your rights over your own information.

Section 03: Defining terms

Privacy is
about power.

You don’t need to be a hacker in a dimly-lit room on the Dark Web to care about privacy. People often confuse three distinct concepts:

Privacy

Data is only seen by who you intend to see it. It is about consent and control.

Security

You can trust the tools you’re using, in the same way you trust your front door and keys to keep you safe.

Anonymity

Acting without being identified. Disconnecting your actions from your real-world identity.

You don’t need all three all the time. Right now, on the modern web, you barely have one.

Section 04: I know where you live

What do we
already know about you?

Before we get into complicated trackers, there is a category of data openly available to anyone with a browser and thirty seconds of free time—the exact data used in the phishing email we just saw.

What's available

  • Full name + age
  • Current & past home addresses
  • Phone numbers
  • Email addresses
  • Family members' names (parents, siblings)
  • Employment history
  • Marriage & divorce records

Where does it come from?

  • Voter registration records (public in most states)
  • Property/housing records (publicly accessible)
  • Court records civil, criminal, probate
  • Old data breaches scraped, compiled, resold

Note: Tax records are not public (though even that norm has been tested recently)

Section 05: Live Demo

Let's look
someone up.

People search sites aggregate public record data into a single profile. They require no login. They're designed for the average consumer, but they are weaponized by stalkers, scammers, and abusers.

We tried it on two Ms. Feng and Mr. Bauld. Here's what came back:

  • Full address history going back years: every place they'd ever lived, including their childhood homes
  • Names of close relatives: parents, siblings, adult children
  • Associated phone numbers and email addresses
  • Their employment history and current employer
  • Enough detail to craft a convincing, personalized attack
Section 06: Why it exists

Why can anyone
access this?

Because in the US, there is no comprehensive federal data privacy law. Aggregating and reselling public records is entirely legal. The companies doing this have spent heavily to keep it that way.

In the EU, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (2018) fundamentally changed this:

  • Companies must have a legal basis to collect data
  • You have the right to be forgotten
  • People search sites of this type cannot legally operate for EU residents

The only reason you can download your Instagram, TikTok, or Google data today is because of GDPR pressure. Without it, we would have essentially no visibility into what these companies collect on us.

US vs. EU

EU
GDPR

Comprehensive federal-level data protection. Data minimization, purpose limitation, right to erasure, mandatory breach notification within 72 hours.

US
No equivalent federal law

Patchwork of sector-specific laws (HIPAA for health, FERPA for education). No general right to erasure. No data minimization requirements. People search sites: legal.

Lobbyists spent hundreds of millions to prevent a US GDPR equivalent.

Section 07: Real Consequences

This is not
an abstract problem.

Domestic Violence

Survivors attempting to rebuild their lives are findable in seconds by abusers using free people-search tools. Shelter locations can be compromised through family member lookups.

Stalking

Journalists, activists, and public figures can be physically located. From TikTokers to teachers, no one is exempt.

Targeted Scams

Armed with your address history, relatives' names, and employer, scammers craft impersonations, like the email we saw at the start, pretending to be a family member in trouble, a bank, or law enforcement.

ICE and Project 2025: Policy proposals explicitly discuss expanding access to commercial data broker databases to assist with immigration enforcement.

Section 08: Browser Tracking

The invisible
follow.

People search is layer one. Layer two is the browser. Your activity across the entire web is tracked — not just on sites you use, but sites you've never heard of.

How tracking works

  • Third-party cookies: Small files that follow you from site to site
  • Tracking pixels: Invisible images embedded in pages and emails that phone home when loaded
  • Browser fingerprinting: Your screen size, fonts, and config form a unique identifier
  • Link decorators: The ?utm_source= strings in URLs

Who is doing it

You are not just tracked on Google and Facebook. Their tracking scripts are embedded across news sites, health sites, and children's content.

When you read an article about a medical condition, search for a lawyer, or lookup information about a protest, that information is logged and permanently added to your profile.

Section 10: The Data Economy

Your data gets
sold.

What data brokers sell

  • Browsing history and search queries
  • Purchase history and financials
  • Location history (precise GPS pings)
  • Health and medical interests

The data broker industry is worth over $300 billion annually. You are the product. You are not paid.

"But I like personalized ads!"

Advertisers do not have your interests in mind, they have conversion rates in mind. They fuel:

  • Planned obsolescence: products designed to break so you buy again
  • Fast fashion and cheaply made goods — fueled by behavioral data about impulse buying
  • Environmental and labor cost of that production chain — paid by workers and ecosystems, not the advertiser

The "personalized ads" framing is designed to make surveillance feel like a service. It isn't. It's an invasion of your privacy.

Section 11: Breaches

And then
it gets stolen.

Even if you trusted every company handling your data — and you shouldn't — the data they collect is an attractive target. Large-scale breaches are a constant, ongoing feature of the current system.

2026
European Commission — 340 GB stolen

Personal data of 29+ EU entities via a supply chain attack.

2025
Hims & Hers — customer support hacked

Telehealth company. Names and contact info exposed via social engineering.

2023
Experian, credit data of millions exposed

The company that holds your credit score, breached again.

2018
Cambridge Analytica / Facebook

87 million users' data harvested without consent and used for political targeting. This event is what prompted serious discussion of GDPR enforcement.

Every company that collects more data than it needs creates a larger target. The solution is collecting less data in the first place.

Section 12: The Structural Problem

This is
by design.

The surveillance infrastructure was built to extract profit from human attention and behavior. Surveillance capitalism refines your behavioral data into predictive products.

Tech companies have lobbied aggressively against privacy legislation in the US. They frame data collection as "improving your experience" and write 10,000-word privacy policies specifically because no one reads them.

The lack of US privacy law is the intentional outcome of an industry that spent enormous sums to prevent regulation.

Who lobbied against GDPR?

Google, Meta, Amazon, and the US Chamber of Commerce lobbied hard against any US equivalent.

Who gets hurt most?

People fleeing domestic abuse. Undocumented immigrants. Journalists. Minors. The people with the least power to push back are the most exposed.

Section 13: Your Data

What do they
actually have on you?

Because of GDPR, platforms are required to allow users to download their data. This is eye-opening.

How to request your data

  • Instagram: Settings → Your Activity → Download Your Information
  • Google: takeout.google.com
  • TikTok: Settings → Privacy → Download Your Data

What you'll typically find

  • Every search query, ever
  • Precise GPS location history going back years
  • Inferred interests and political leanings

If you can download it — imagine what they're not showing you.

Section 15: Tools

More tools
worth knowing.

Password Manager

Bitwarden

Free, open source. Generates and stores strong unique passwords to prevent credential stuffing after breaches.

VPN

Mullvad / Proton VPN

Encrypts your internet traffic from your ISP. Mullvad accepts cash and keeps no logs.

Encrypted Messaging

Signal

E2E encrypted by default. Open source. WhatsApp shares metadata. iMessage is Apple-only.

Opt-out

Remove yourself

Services like DeleteMe automate removal from hundreds of data brokers for a fee.

Cookie Management

Reject non-essential

Find the "manage preferences" option and disable analytics and advertising cookies.

DNS

Encrypted DNS

Setting DNS to Quad9 or 1.1.1.1 encrypts domain lookups from your ISP.

Section 16: Collective Action

Individual changes
are not enough.

Individual solutions to structural problems are inherently limited. Not everyone has the resources to navigate this. The people who can't remain exposed.

The goal should be that surveillance capitalism is constrained by law, the way other forms of exploitation are constrained by law.

What political action looks like

  • Support federal privacy legislation
  • Oppose legislation weakening encryption
  • Push back on ICE data broker access

Closing: The Bigger Picture
∑

Measurement
as control.

Vincent's point about measurement applies with to the data economy. You are being measured constantly. Because the US has no meaningful privacy law, you have almost no right to stop it.

Who should be in control of this measurement? The companies extracting profit from it? Or the people whose lives are being quantified?

Privacy isn't about having something to hide. It's about who gets to hold the measuring stick.
