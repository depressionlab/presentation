Obligatory Book Tie-In

Measurement and Profit.

James Vincent's Beyond Measure is about our obsession with quantifying the world. It traces how humans have used measurement to make sense of the universe, but warns of the danger of reducing complex human experiences into mere data points.

Measurement is never neutral. Whoever controls what gets measured holds power.

An Inevitable Outcome

Surveillance capitalism is an inevitable outcome as we continue to prioritize measurement and profit to its extremes. It is the process of taking a human being with complexities and desires, and measuring them down to a spreadsheet of clickable behaviors to be categorized and sold to the highest bidder.

Section 01: The Philosophy

The "Free"
Web (Web 2.0)

Privacy is about what is taken from us silently.

Data privacy can seem suspicious, but it's not actually about hiding dirty secrets. Data privacy is better seen as resisting the capitalist commodification of our human experiences.

Data Minimization

The concept that a company should only collect the absolute minimum amount of data required to provide a service. If a flashlight app needs your GPS location, that should raise alarm bells.

Section 02: The Myth

"I have nothing
to hide."

There is a phrase that kills more privacy legislation than even the biggest tech lobby could dream of: "I have nothing to hide." It's a comforting thought. But what does it mean?

We all know what happens in bathrooms. It's not a secret. There are no classified documents protecting toilets and sinks. But you still close the door.

You don't close the door because you're doing something wrong; you close the door because existing in a space in which you’re constantly observed strips you of your dignity and humanity.

This is because you want privacy, not secrecy.
Privacy is the boundary between being a human and being a specimen.

Section 03: Defining terms

Privacy is
about power.

You don't need to be a hacker in a dimly-lit room to care about privacy.

Privacy

Data is only seen by who you intend to see it. It is entirely about consent and control.

Security

You can trust the tools you're using. Your data is protected against unauthorized access or breaches.

Anonymity

Acting without being identified. Disconnecting your digital actions from your real-world identity.

You don't need all three all the time. Right now, on the modern web, you barely have one.

Section 04: Public Records

What do we
already know about you?

Before we get into complicated browser trackers, there is data openly available to anyone with a browser and thirty seconds of free time—the exact data used in the phishing demo.

Available for free

  • Full name + exact age
  • Current & past home addresses
  • Phone numbers & emails
  • Family members' names

Where does it come from?

  • Voter registration records
  • Property/housing records
  • Court & DMV records
  • Old data breaches scraped and compiled
Section 05: Data Brokers

The People-Search
Industry.

People-search sites aggregate public record data into a single, highly readable profile. They require no login. They are routinely weaponized by stalkers, scammers, and abusers.

When we looked up a random adult, here is what came back instantly:

  • Full address history going back 15 years, including childhood homes.
  • Names of close relatives: parents, siblings, and adult children.
  • Their employment history and current employer.
This provides enough detail to craft a convincing, highly personalized attack on anyone.
Section 06: Why is this legal?

A failure of
legislation.

In the US, there is no comprehensive federal data privacy law. Aggregating and reselling public records is entirely legal.

In the EU, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) fundamentally changed this:

  • Companies must have a legal basis to collect data.
  • You have the right to be forgotten.
  • People-search sites cannot legally operate.

> The only reason you can download your Instagram or TikTok data today is because of global GDPR pressure.

Regulatory Landscape

EU
GDPR (2018)

Comprehensive protection. Mandates data minimization, purpose limitation, and breach notification.

US
No Federal Equivalent

A patchwork of sector-specific laws. No general right to erasure. People search sites are fully legal.

Section 07: Real Consequences

This is not
an abstract problem.

Domestic Violence

Survivors attempting to rebuild their lives are findable in seconds by abusers. Confidential shelter locations are often compromised.

Stalking

Journalists, activists, and public figures can be physically located instantly. From creators to school teachers, no one is exempt.

Targeted Scams

Armed with address history and relatives' names, scammers craft impersonations pretending to be a family member in trouble.

Law Enforcement Bypass: Government agencies are explicitly purchasing data broker databases to bypass the need for warrants. If they can buy your location data from an app developer, they don't need a judge's permission.

Section 08: Browser Tracking

The invisible
follow.

People search is layer one. Layer two is the browser. Your activity across the entire web is tracked — on sites you use, and sites you've never heard of.

How tracking works

  • Third-party cookies: Files that follow you site to site.
  • Tracking pixels: Invisible 1x1 images embedded in pages and emails that log when you open them.
  • Browser fingerprinting: Your screen size, fonts, and hardware config form a unique identifier.

Who is doing it?

You are not just tracked while on Google or Meta. Their tracking scripts are invisibly embedded across news sites, health forums, and children's content.

When you read an article about a medical condition, or search for a bankruptcy lawyer, that information is logged and added to your shadow profile.

Section 10: The Data Economy

Your data gets
sold.

What data brokers package

  • Browsing history and search queries.
  • Purchase history and estimated financial status.
  • Precise GPS location history.
  • Inferred health conditions and medical interests.

The data broker industry is worth over $300 billion annually. You are the product. You are not paid.

"But I like personalized ads!"

Advertisers do not have your interests in mind, they have conversion rates in mind. They use your behavioral profiles to fuel:

  • Planned obsolescence.
  • Impulse buying: triggered by emotional state profiling.

The "personalized ads" framing is a marketing spin designed to make surveillance feel like a helpful service.

Section 11: Security Failures

And then
it gets stolen.

Even if you trusted every company handling your data, massive hoards of data are attractive targets. Breaches are an ongoing feature of the system.

2024
National Public Data Breach

Nearly 3 billion records containing SSNs and addresses leaked. A reminder that brokers fail to secure scraped data.

2023
23andMe

Massive exposures of highly sensitive genetic information and ancestry data due to credential stuffing.

2018
Cambridge Analytica

87 million users' psychological data harvested without consent and utilized for targeted political manipulation.

Section 12: The Architecture

This is happening
by design.

The surveillance infrastructure was built intentionally to extract maximum profit from human attention and behavior.

Tech monopolies frame data collection as "improving your experience" while burying the truth in 10,000-word privacy policies designed to be unreadable.

The lack of a US privacy law is the intentional outcome of an industry protecting its business model.

Who blocked laws?

Google, Meta, and massive advertising coalitions lobbied tirelessly against giving citizens basic data rights.

Who gets hurt most?

People fleeing abuse, undocumented immigrants, activists, and minors. Those with the least power are the most exposed.

Section 13: Look For Yourself

What do they
actually have on you?

Because of the GDPR, major platforms are legally required to allow users to download their personal data archives. Doing this is eye-opening.

How to request your data

  • Instagram: Settings → Your Activity → Download Your Information
  • Google: takeout.google.com
  • Apple: privacy.apple.com

What you'll typically find

  • Every search query you've ever typed.
  • Precise GPS location history going back years.
  • Highly specific inferred interests, political leanings, and estimated income.
Section 14: Practical Steps

Things that
actually help.

Privacy exists on a spectrum. Don't let perfection be the enemy of progress. Start here:

01

Use an Adblocker

uBlock Origin blocks trackers web-wide. The highest-impact action you can take.

02

Switch Browsers

Firefox or Brave. Chrome exists specifically to feed Google's ad platform.

03

Change Default Search

DuckDuckGo does not build a profile on you. Google Search logs every query.

04

Move from Gmail

Proton Mail is end-to-end encrypted. Google scans your habits from your inbox.

05

Use Email Aliases

Stop giving out your real email. Use SimpleLogin to prevent cross-site tracking.

06

Enable 2FA (MFA)

Use an Authenticator app to severely limit the damage of inevitable password breaches.

Encrypted Messaging

Signal

End-to-End encrypted by default. Non-profit. WhatsApp shares metadata with Meta. iMessage locks you to Apple.

Data Broker Opt-out

Remove Yourself

Services like DeleteMe or Kanary automate the grueling process of removing your profile from brokers.

Cookie Management

Reject Non-Essential

Always find the "manage preferences" option and disable analytics and advertising cookies. Never click "Accept All".

The goal must be that surveillance capitalism is constrained by law, the exact same way other forms of human exploitation are constrained by law.
